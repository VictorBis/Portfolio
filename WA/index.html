What is the Wellbeing Assessment?

A four-pillar questionnaire designed to tailor the Alight Worklife experience to users' needs

The Wellbeing Assessment covers Mind, Body, Wallet, and Life, personalizing the platform based on responses to offer tailored content, recommendations, and action items for better work-life balance over time.

Product Objectives

Using the Eigenquestions technique, we gathered strategic questions whose answers would help us better understand the business needs

We had many questions about the product, but to optimize our time, we used the Eigenquestions technique. This helped us identify key, consequential questions that would either answer all others or make them easier to address.

The Problem

How might we make the assessment easy and engaging for Worklife users so that they complete the entire questionnaire without dropping off?

The assessment is a lengthy questionnaire, with some pillars asking for sensitive information, increasing the likelihood of user drop-off. However, gathering all this data is crucial for the algorithm to deliver a more personalized and engaging experience, which enhances user satisfaction, improves client outcomes, and potentially attracts new clients.

The Goal

How do we measure success? By the percentage of users who complete the assessment

Previous assessments had a completion rate of less than 10% without incentives, which increased to 50% when incentives were offered.

Scope

Before starting, we needed a clear definition of our requirements

After meeting with the PM, we defined the fundamental requirements of the project as follows:

  • Design an assessment that allows users to navigate through all predefined questions and gain insights from their answers.
  • Ensure that all predefined questions, which are integral to the algorithm, are included in the final product.
  • Focus solely on the assessment; the scope does not cover the flow leading users to it, how users are invited to complete it, or how results are reflected on the platform.

Relationship

Defining our core objectives and understing the purpose of the questions and its intended use

Based on the three rules that influence response rates, we defined the elements where we would focus:

  • Establish trust
    • Ensure the form has a clear purpose and communicates it effectively.
    • Ensure the form looks professionally designed.
    • Ensure the form functions correctly, with no defects or typographical errors.
  • Reduce social costs
    • Keep the form easy and effortless to fill out.
    • Help users feel in control of the form.
    • Minimize requests for sensitive or personal information.
    • Design questions that are easy for users to answer.
  • Increase rewards
    • Clearly communicate the reward for completing the form.

Conversation

Analyzing questions, writing instructions, choosing form controls, and defining the assessment's overall structure

At this stage, we focused on analizing and organizing the assessment content to ensure it was easy to understand, complete, and navigate.

Making questions easy to answer

We analized every single question of the assessment to identify:

  • The answer format.
  • Use of familiar concepts.
  • Whether only one question is asked at a time.
  • Use of positive language.
  • Need for supportive text.
  • Request for too private information.

As a result, we proposed improvements based on the findings to enhance question quality.

Through research and collaboration with the dev team, we defined sizes and behavior for all size levels

Few apps support dynamic typography, but by reviewing the available ones, we identified how it works and common practices.

Since supporting all levels required extensive testing, which the dev team couldn't manage at the time, we grouped some levels to use the same behavior.

Final Steps

With the final components complete, we shared key templates with the dev team and established guidelines for the design team

Many app screens shared similar layouts but with different content. By providing the dev team with key templates, they could migrate the entire app without needing mockups for every screen available. For screens that didn't fit a template, the design team created them.

Some key flows were going to be updated using the new design language before and after the app's update launch. To ensure consistency, we created design guidelines for AWLDS focused on the app, and I reviewed and approved new designs to ensure they aligned with the app's standards.
